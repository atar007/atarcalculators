How your HSC marks become an ATAR

Two organisations produce two different numbers. NESA reports your HSC marks. UAC turns those marks into an ATAR. Nothing printed on your HSC results notice is your ATAR, and no band on it is a rank. This page follows one mark through both stages.

Stage 1 — where an HSC mark comes from

Each HSC mark out of 100 is an average of two halves. One half is your school assessment mark, moderated against how your cohort performed in the written exam. The other half is your exam mark. Averaging the two gives the mark on your results notice, and that mark is what this calculator asks you to enter.

This matters because your assessment mark is not discarded once exams begin. A student who is strong all year and average in the exam, and a student who is the reverse, can finish on the same HSC mark.

Stage 2 — what UAC does with it

Scaled mark (per unit) = your HSC mark, adjusted for the strength of that course's cohort Aggregate = your best 10 units of scaled marks, added together (out of 500) ATAR = where that aggregate places you against your state cohort (0 to 99.95) Each HSC course carries 2 units, except Extension courses, which carry 1. At least 2 units of English must sit inside your best 10.

Why units, not subjects, are the real currency

The aggregate counts units, not subject names. A 2-unit course contributes its scaled mark twice. A 1-unit Extension course contributes once. So ten units might be five ordinary courses, or four courses plus two Extension units. Because the aggregate keeps only your best ten units, a weak eleventh and twelfth unit simply fall out and cost you nothing.

Worked example

  1. Start with marks. Six courses, each marked out of 100, plus one Extension course marked out of 50.
  2. Scale each one. Every mark is converted using that course's scaling for the year. Two students on 88 in different courses will not land on the same scaled mark.
  3. Spread them across units. Each 2-unit course's scaled mark is counted twice; the Extension unit once.
  4. Keep the best ten units. English is locked in first, then the highest remaining units fill the rest.
  5. Add and rank. Those ten scaled units add to an aggregate out of 500, and that aggregate is placed against every other student to produce the ATAR.

The estimate above runs exactly this sequence on the marks you enter.

What this calculator does with your HSC marks

You enter a mark for each course. The calculator scales each one using UAC's published scaling data, spreads the scaled marks across their unit weights, enforces the English requirement, keeps the best ten units, and ranks the resulting aggregate. It is the same sequence UAC follows, run on the numbers you supply.

Course coverage includes the full HSC course list, with Extension courses handled at their correct 1-unit weight rather than being treated like a 2-unit course. That distinction is the one most hand-made spreadsheets get wrong.

What it cannot do

Scaling is recalculated every year against the actual cohort, and this year's cohort does not exist until the exams are sat. Any calculator, including this one, is therefore working from the most recent published scaling rather than the scaling that will apply to your year. Treat the output as a well-grounded estimate, not a prediction.

It also cannot see your school, your cohort, or your assessment ranks. It only knows the marks you type in.

What throws an HSC estimate off

  • Entering a band instead of a mark. A band is a range, not a number. Entering "Band 6" as 90 when you are tracking closer to 97 changes the aggregate substantially. Use the mark, not the label.
  • Counting an Extension course as 2 units. Extension courses are 1 unit. Counting one twice inflates the aggregate and produces an estimate that will not arrive.
  • Adding bonus points to the result. Adjustment factors do not touch your ATAR. They adjust your selection rank for particular courses at particular universities, after your ATAR exists.
  • Using an exam mark alone. Your HSC mark blends the exam with your moderated assessment mark. Entering a raw exam estimate skips half the calculation.
  • Reading the last decimal seriously. The honest resolution of any pre-results estimate is a point or two either side, because the scaling that will apply has not been calculated yet.

What a Band 6 means for your HSC ATAR

A Band 6 is an HSC mark of 90 to 100 in a 2-unit course, and it is what many HSC students aim for. But a Band 6 is not the same as your ATAR. Your ATAR comes from your scaled marks and where they place you against the whole state, not from the band labels on your results.

Because of scaling, a Band 6 in a subject with a strong cohort can lift your ATAR more than a Band 6 in a subject with a weaker one. So two students can both earn a Band 6 in a subject and see it contribute differently, depending on how that subject scales.

For your ATAR, what matters is your scaled mark in each of your best 10 units, including your two units of English. The calculator above works from your marks to estimate that outcome. Chasing a Band 6 in every subject is a fine goal, and understanding how each subject scales shows you which marks move your ATAR the most. For the official picture, NESA reports your bands and UAC calculates your ATAR.

How HSC subject scaling shapes your ATAR

Not every HSC subject scales the same way. UAC scales each subject against the strength of its cohort, so subjects taken by more competitive students, like the Extension maths and sciences, tend to scale up, while others scale down. Scaling acts on your rank within each course, not on the raw mark alone.

This matters because two students with the same raw marks in different subjects can end up with different ATARs. It does not mean you should chase high-scaling subjects you dislike, since a strong result in a subject that suits you usually beats a weak result in a harder-scaling one.

The calculator above already factors scaling into its estimate. To see how a specific subject behaves, our HSC subject scaling guides explain each one, from Physics and Chemistry to English Advanced and Maths Extension 1.

HSC marks and the ATAR — common questions

Is my HSC mark the same as my exam mark?

No. Your HSC mark for a course is the average of your exam mark and your school assessment mark, after that assessment mark has been moderated against your cohort's exam performance. Both halves count.

What is the difference between an HSC mark and a scaled mark?

Your HSC mark is what NESA reports, out of 100. Your scaled mark is what UAC produces after adjusting that mark for the academic strength of the cohort that took the course. Only scaled marks enter the aggregate.

How many units is each HSC course?

Every HSC course carries 2 units, except Extension courses, which carry 1 unit each. Your ATAR is built from your best 10 units.

Do Extension courses count as one unit or two?

One. An Extension course contributes its scaled mark once, not twice. This is why Extension units behave differently in the aggregate from ordinary courses.

What is the aggregate out of 500?

It is the sum of your best 10 units of scaled marks. Each unit can contribute up to 50 scaled marks, so ten units give a maximum of 500. That total is what gets ranked.

Can two students with the same Band 6 end up with different ATARs?

Yes, easily. A band is a 10-mark range, so two Band 6 students can sit 9 marks apart. On top of that, the same mark in two different courses scales differently, because scaling reflects the strength of each course's cohort.

Does my school assessment mark affect my ATAR?

Indirectly, but genuinely. It forms half of your HSC mark, and your HSC mark is what gets scaled. A strong assessment record cushions a weaker exam.

What happens if I take more than 10 units?

Only your best 10 units count. Extra units are not wasted, though: they give the calculation more to choose from, so a weak unit can simply drop out of the aggregate.

Why does English have to count?

The rule is fixed: at least 2 units of an English course must be inside your best 10, whichever English course you take. It is the only compulsory component of the aggregate.
