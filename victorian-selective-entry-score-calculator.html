How the Victorian selective entry score works

Entry to Victoria’s four selective entry high schools is decided by one exam covering five areas, run for the state by ACER. Here is how the score is built.

The formula

Entry score = the five ACER test sections, combined Sections cover verbal and numerical reasoning, reading, maths and writing One exam result is used to rank you across your school preferences Victoria uses a single common exam for all four schools (Melbourne High, Mac.Robertson, Nossal and Suzanne Cory). Your one result is ranked against your listed preferences; there is no separate test per school.

Step by step

  1. Sit the one exam. The ACER exam covers verbal reasoning, numerical reasoning, reading comprehension, mathematics, and writing.
  2. Each section is scored. The sections are marked and scaled so they combine fairly into one overall result.
  3. Your sections are combined. The scaled sections are brought together into a single entry score.
  4. Places are offered by rank. Your one score is matched against the schools you preference, ranked against every other applicant.

The four selective entry high schools

Around 1,000 Year 9 places are available each year across the four schools. That is against roughly 5,000 to 6,000 applicants, an acceptance rate near 16 to 20%. You rank all four schools in preference order. The process then places you at the highest-ranked school where your ranking is competitive.

Reasoning over memorisation

Since 2023 ACER runs the exam, and it no longer requires knowledge beyond Year 8. The five sections reward genuine reasoning, reading and problem-solving under tight time pressure rather than memorised content. Numerical Reasoning, in particular, tests how you think rather than what you know.

One exam, four schools, ranked preferences

You sit a single exam and nominate all four schools in order. Because the schools differ in competitiveness, your preference order matters. Listing a slightly less competitive school can secure a place that a top-only list would miss.

Different from NSW

Victoria has only four selective schools and entry is at Year 9, unlike the NSW system with around 47 schools and Year 7 entry. If you are in NSW, use the NSW selective test calculator instead.

Victoria's select-entry high schools use a common entrance exam administered through the Victorian Government, which sets the official process.

Common mistakes with Victorian selective entry

These are the misunderstandings families most often have.

  • Thinking each school has its own test. Victoria uses one common ACER exam for all four selective schools. You sit it once.
  • Expecting a published cut-off. Scores needed change each year with the applicant pool. Any figure online is an estimate.
  • Assuming it works like NSW. The section mix, timing and provider differ from the NSW selective test. Prepare for the Victorian format specifically.
  • Neglecting the writing section. Writing is part of the exam. Strong reasoning scores do not make up for a weak written piece.
  • Treating the estimate as final. This is indicative. The official result and offers come from the schools after the exam.

Victorian selective entry — common questions

What's on the Victorian selective entry exam?

Five sections: Verbal Reasoning, Numerical Reasoning, Reading Comprehension, Mathematics, and Writing. Since 2023 it is set and run by ACER, and it tests reasoning and aptitude rather than content beyond Year 8.

How is the Victorian exam scored?

Results form a composite ranking used to place students. There is no published pass mark; selection is competitive ranking, and you list all four schools in preference order.

Is this calculator the official score?

No. It is an indicative self-assessment across the five sections. Only ACER and the Victorian Department of Education issue the official result and offers.

Which Victorian selective schools are there?

Four: Melbourne High School (boys), Mac.Robertson Girls' High School (girls), Nossal High School (co-ed), and Suzanne Cory High School (co-ed). Students sit the exam in Year 8 for Year 9 entry.

What ranking do I need to get in?

There is no fixed cut-off. Melbourne High and Mac.Robertson generally require the highest rankings, while Suzanne Cory and Nossal may take students from slightly lower percentiles. About 16 to 20% of applicants receive offers.

How many places are there?

Around 1,000 Year 9 places across the four schools each year, against roughly 5,000 to 6,000 applicants.
