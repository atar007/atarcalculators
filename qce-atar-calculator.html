How your QCE results become an ATAR

Queensland splits the job between two bodies. QCAA runs your subjects and reports your results. QTAC takes those results and produces the ATAR. Your QCE certificate and your ATAR are separate things: you can complete one without the other.

Stage 1 — where a subject result comes from

Each General subject gives you a result reported on a 0 to 100 scale. That result combines your internal assessments, marked through the year, with your external assessment at the end. Both parts feed the single number QTAC later works from.

Stage 2 — what QTAC does with it

Scaled result = your subject result (out of 100), scaled by QTAC TEA = your best 5 scaled results, added together (out of 500) ATAR = your TEA converted to a rank (0 to 99.95) English is compulsory: you need at least one English subject at a C or higher. QTAC counts your best five scaled results in full, with no partial increments.

Why five, and why "in full"

Queensland's method is unusually clean. There is no tapering and no fractional contribution: your best five scaled results each count completely, and everything beyond the fifth is set aside. A sixth subject is therefore insurance rather than an addition — it can replace a weaker result, but it cannot top up your total.

Worked example

  1. Collect results. Six General subjects, each on the 0 to 100 scale.
  2. Scale each one. QTAC adjusts every result for the strength of that subject's cohort, so identical results in two subjects rarely stay identical.
  3. Rank them. The five highest scaled results are kept and the sixth drops out.
  4. Add to a TEA. Those five scaled results sum to a total out of 500.
  5. Convert to a rank. Your TEA is placed against the state cohort to give the ATAR.

What this calculator does with your QCE results

You enter a result for each subject. The calculator scales each one, checks the English requirement, keeps your best five scaled results, sums them into a TEA out of 500, and converts that to a rank. That is QTAC's sequence, run on your numbers.

Because Queensland counts five results in full, the arithmetic is more transparent than in states that use increments — but the scaling step still does the heavy lifting, and that is the part you cannot do in your head.

What it cannot do

Scaling is recalculated each year against the cohort that actually sat the subjects, so no calculator can know this year's scaling in advance. The estimate uses the most recent published data. Expect a point or two of movement either side, and treat it as a planning number.

What throws a QCE estimate off

  • Assuming your sixth subject adds to the total. It does not. Only your best five scaled results count, and they count in full. A sixth subject can only replace a weaker one.
  • Confusing the QCE with the ATAR. The QCE is a certificate showing you met Queensland's senior schooling requirements. The ATAR is a separate rank for tertiary entry. Completing the QCE does not by itself produce a competitive ATAR.
  • Forgetting the English requirement. You need at least one English subject at a C or higher for an ATAR, regardless of how strong your other results are.
  • Entering a predicted internal mark as a subject result. Your subject result combines internal and external assessment. Using only your internal marks skips the exam half.
  • Expecting an exact number. The scaling that will apply to your cohort has not been calculated yet, so a point or two of margin is honest.

How your QCE results feed your ATAR

Under the QCE system, you complete General subjects assessed through internal assessments and an external exam. Your subject results are scaled by QTAC so results from different subjects can be compared fairly, and your best five scaled results are combined into the aggregate that becomes your ATAR.

Your QCE is the certificate showing you have met Queensland's senior schooling requirements, while your ATAR is the separate rank used for tertiary entry. You can complete your QCE without necessarily achieving a competitive ATAR, since the two measure different things.

Because scaling depends on each subject's cohort, a strong result in one subject may contribute differently from the same result in another. The calculator above estimates your ATAR from your results, and QTAC calculates the official ATAR each year.

General and Applied subjects under the QCE

In Queensland, subjects fall into different types, and this affects your ATAR. General subjects, assessed with internal assessments and an external exam, are the ones that contribute to an ATAR. Applied subjects and some other courses count towards your QCE but are treated differently for tertiary entry.

To build a competitive ATAR, most students take a set of General subjects and aim for strong scaled results across them. Your QCE can still be completed with a mix of subject types, since the certificate and the ATAR have different requirements.

The calculator above estimates your ATAR from your results. When choosing subjects, it helps to check which are General subjects and how each is likely to be scaled, since that shapes how your results translate into a rank.

QCE results and the ATAR — common questions

Is the QCE the same as the ATAR?

No. The QCE is the certificate that shows you met Queensland's senior schooling requirements. The ATAR is a separate rank used for tertiary entry. You can finish the QCE without an ATAR, and the two measure different things.

How many subjects count towards my Queensland ATAR?

Five. QTAC adds your best five scaled results in full to build your TEA. Results beyond the fifth are set aside.

Does a sixth subject increase my ATAR?

It cannot add to your total, because only five results count. What it can do is act as insurance: if it scales better than one of your top five, it takes that place.

What is a TEA in Queensland?

Your TEA is the sum of your best five scaled results, out of 500. It is the total that gets converted into your ATAR rank.

What is the difference between my subject result and my scaled result?

Your subject result is the 0 to 100 figure QCAA reports. Your scaled result is what QTAC produces after adjusting for the strength of that subject's cohort. Only scaled results enter the TEA.

Do internal assessments count, or just the external exam?

Both. Your subject result combines your internal assessments across the year with your external assessment at the end, and that combined result is what gets scaled.

Do I need English for a Queensland ATAR?

Yes. You need at least one English subject completed at a C or higher. It is a requirement for the ATAR to be issued.

Why do two students with the same result get different scaled results?

Because they took different subjects. Scaling reflects the academic strength of each subject's cohort, so the same 80 in two subjects can land on two different scaled results.
