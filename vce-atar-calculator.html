How your VCE study scores become an ATAR

Victoria runs the process through two bodies. VCAA reports your study scores. VTAC turns them into an ATAR. Your study scores are not ranks, and no single study score is your ATAR.

Stage 1 — where a study score comes from

Each Unit 3&4 study gives you a study score from 0 to 50. It combines your coursework across the year with your exams, and it already positions you against everyone else who took that study. A study score of 30 sits around the middle of that group.

Stage 2 — what VTAC does with it

Scaled study score = your VCE study score (out of 50), scaled by VTAC Aggregate = your best 4 scaled scores, plus 10% of your 5th and 6th ATAR = your aggregate (out of about 210) converted to a rank (0 to 99.95) English is compulsory: one English study must be in your calculation. VTAC counts your top four studies in full — the "Primary Four" — then adds a 10% increment from up to two more.

Why the 5th and 6th behave differently

This is the part of the Victorian system most often misread. Your fifth and sixth studies do not count like the first four. Each contributes only a tenth of its scaled score. A scaled 40 in your fifth study adds 4 to the aggregate, not 40. They are worth having, and they are not worth sacrificing your Primary Four for.

Worked example

  1. Collect study scores. Six Unit 3&4 studies, each scored out of 50.
  2. Scale each one. VTAC adjusts every study score for the strength of that study's cohort.
  3. Pick the Primary Four. English is included, then the highest remaining scaled scores fill the four, counting in full.
  4. Add the increments. A tenth of the fifth and sixth scaled scores is added on top.
  5. Convert to a rank. The aggregate, out of roughly 210, is ranked against the state to give the ATAR.

What this calculator does with your study scores

You enter a study score for each study. The calculator scales each one, builds your Primary Four with English included, adds the 10% increments from your fifth and sixth studies, and ranks the aggregate. That is VTAC's sequence, run on your numbers.

The increment rule is handled correctly, which matters: adding a fifth study score in full is the single most common way a hand-made estimate ends up far too high.

What it cannot do

Scaling is recalculated each year against the cohort that actually sat each study, so this year's scaling does not exist yet. The estimate uses the most recent published data and is best read with a point or two of margin.

What throws a VCE estimate off

  • Counting the 5th and 6th studies in full. They contribute 10% each, not 100%. This single error inflates an estimate more than any other.
  • Treating a study score of 30 as a poor result. A 30 sits around the middle of that study's cohort. It is not a failure, and four solid middling scores plus increments still build a real aggregate.
  • Leaving English out of the Primary Four. An English study must be in the calculation. Building a Primary Four without one does not describe any real outcome.
  • Assuming a scaled score replaces the study score. VCAA still reports your study score; the scaled version exists only inside VTAC's aggregate. Both numbers are real, and they answer different questions.
  • Reading the last decimal seriously. The scaling for your cohort has not been calculated yet.

How your VCE study scores become your ATAR

In the VCE, each subject gives you a study score out of 50, set so the state average is about 30. Your study scores are not your ATAR, though. VTAC scales each study score against how the whole subject cohort performed, then combines your scaled scores into an aggregate, which becomes your ATAR.

Your aggregate uses your best scaled scores, and one of your English studies must be included. The primary four, English plus your best three, carry the most weight, with a 10 per cent contribution from your fifth and sixth studies.

This is why a study score of 40 in one subject can move your ATAR differently from a 40 in another: the scaling depends on the strength of each cohort. The calculator above estimates your ATAR from your study scores, and the official scaling and ATAR come from VTAC each year.

How VCE subject scaling shapes your ATAR

Not every VCE study scales the same way. VTAC scales each study score against the strength of its cohort, so studies taken by more competitive students, like Specialist Maths, Methods and the sciences, tend to scale up, while others scale down. Scaling reflects the cohort, not a difficulty bonus.

This is why the same study score in two different subjects can affect your ATAR differently. It does not mean you should pick subjects only for scaling, since doing well in a subject that suits you usually matters more than a small scaling gain.

The calculator above already factors scaling into its estimate. To see how a specific study behaves, our VCE subject scaling guides cover each one, from Methods and Specialist Maths to Chemistry and English.

VCE study scores and the ATAR — common questions

Is a study score of 30 a bad result?

No. A study score of 30 sits around the middle of the group that took that study. It is an ordinary, solid result rather than a poor one, and a set of scores around 30 still produces a real aggregate.

How much do my 5th and 6th studies actually count?

A tenth each. Your best four scaled scores count in full, then 10% of your fifth and 10% of your sixth are added. A scaled 40 in your fifth study adds 4 to the aggregate.

What is the Primary Four?

It is your top four scaled study scores, which count in full towards your aggregate. An English study must be among the studies in your calculation.

What is the difference between my study score and my scaled study score?

Your study score is what VCAA reports, out of 50. Your scaled study score is what VTAC produces after adjusting for the strength of that study's cohort. Only the scaled version enters your aggregate.

Why is the aggregate out of about 210?

Four scaled scores counting in full give up to 200, and 10% increments from a fifth and sixth add up to roughly 10 more. That total is what gets converted into a rank.

Do I have to do English?

Yes. One English study must be in your ATAR calculation, whichever English study you take.

Does taking a sixth study help?

A little, and only through the 10% increment. It is not worth taking a sixth study if it drags down the four that count in full.

Why did my scaled score differ from my study score?

Because VTAC adjusts each study score for how strong that study's cohort was. Some studies scale up, others scale down, and the size of the shift depends on the group, not on how hard the study felt.
