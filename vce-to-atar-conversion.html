How VCE marks convert to ATAR

Your VCE results and your ATAR are not the same thing. This guide walks through exactly how your VCE study scores turn into an ATAR, step by step, in plain English.

By the ATARCalculators Editorial Team ·Reviewed 4 July 2026 ·Updated July 2026 ·6 min read

VCE study scores are scaled by VTAC to account for how strong each subject’s cohort is, combined into an aggregate, and ranked into an ATAR between 0.00 and 99.95. So your ATAR reflects your rank against your age group, not the raw average of your marks. VCAA runs the Victorian Certificate of Education; VTAC works out your ATAR.

Key takeaways

  • Your ATAR is a rank from 0.00 to 99.95, not the average of your VCE results.
  • VTAC scales each subject, based on how strong its cohort is.
  • Your English study score, best three others, plus 10% of two more form your aggregate.
  • That aggregate is ranked against your age group to give your ATAR.
  • VCAA runs the Victorian Certificate of Education; VTAC works out your ATAR.
  • Each study is assessed by coursework during the year plus external exams.
  • You cannot average your marks to get your ATAR.

A rank, not an average

The first thing to understand is that your ATAR is a rank. An ATAR of 85 does not mean your marks averaged 85. It means you finished ahead of about 85% of your age group.

Because it is a rank, it lets universities compare students who took completely different subjects. That is the whole point of the ATAR. Your results feed into it, but they are not it.

Keeping this distinction clear makes everything else about the VCE and the ATAR easier to follow. Marks describe subjects; the ATAR ranks students.

What the VCE is

The VCE is your certificate; the ATAR is a rank derived from it. Completing the VCE does not produce an ATAR by itself — you need scored studies, including an English.

How a subject result is built

Your VCE result is a set of study scores out of 50, with 30 near the middle. Those are the raw material VTAC works with, and none of them is an ATAR.

How your assessment is handled

Each VCE study score combines your SACs with your exams. The GAT sits behind that, moderating school assessment across the state.

The General Achievement Test

All VCE students sit the General Achievement Test, or GAT. It does not directly count towards your study scores.

Instead, VCAA uses it to check and moderate assessments across the state, so it is worth taking seriously even though it is not scored like a subject.

Step 1: Your subjects are scaled

Once you have your study scores, VTAC scales them. Scaling adjusts each subject so that a result in one subject is worth the same as the same result in another.

It works by looking at how the students in a subject perform across all their subjects. If the group is strong overall, the subject scales up. If the group is broad, it scales down.

Crucially, your position within the subject never changes. Scaling shifts the whole subject up or down; it only changes how your result counts towards your ATAR.

Why scaling exists

Scaling exists to be fair. Without it, a student who took easier subjects would have an advantage over one who took harder, more competitive subjects.

Scaling removes that edge. It means no student is better or worse off simply because of the subjects they chose. That fairness is the whole reason it exists.

This is why the idea that “hard subjects scale up” is only half true. A subject scales up because its students are strong, not because the content is difficult. See VCE scaling explained.

Step 2: Your aggregate

Your VCE aggregate takes your Primary Four scaled scores in full, plus 10% of your fifth and sixth. The maximum sits near 210 — a much smaller number than NSW’s 500.

The English requirement

An English study must be in your VCE calculation. It is the only compulsory element, and it cannot be swapped out however well your other studies scale.

Step 3: Your aggregate becomes a rank

The final step orders every Victorian aggregate and turns your position into a rank out of 99.95. This is where the aggregate stops being a score and becomes a percentile.

A worked example

Work the example with the Victorian numbers: four studies in full, two at 10%, aggregate near 210. Change one Primary Four score and watch the aggregate move — change the sixth and barely anything happens.

Subject results are not your ATAR

A study score of 40 is not an ATAR of 40. They are different scales measuring different things, and confusing them is the commonest Victorian mistake.

Who does what in Victoria

Two bodies are involved. the Victorian Curriculum and Assessment Authority (VCAA) runs the VCE: it sets your subjects, runs the assessment, and gives you your results.

the Victorian Tertiary Admissions Centre (VTAC) takes those results, scales them, and works out your ATAR. So VCAA gives you your VCE; VTAC gives you your ATAR. See VTAC explained.

How many subjects should you take?

Victoria counts six studies in total: four in full and two at a tenth each. That structure is what makes the Primary Four decision so consequential.

Turning your estimate into a plan

Plan around your Primary Four. Your fifth and sixth studies contribute 10% each — useful insurance, and never a rescue.

What scaling does to a result

The 10% increments are genuinely marginal. A brilliant sixth study adds a fraction of what a mediocre Primary Four study costs you.

Why you cannot game the system

You cannot game VCE scaling, because it follows your rank within a study. Moving to a better scaler you rank lower in leaves you worse off.

Subject results are not band labels

Victoria has no bands. Study scores run to 50 and the ATAR runs to 99.95 — if someone mentions a Band 6, they are describing the NSW system.

Why you cannot calculate it exactly

No published table converts a set of study scores into an exact ATAR, because scaling changes with each year’s cohort. Any tool, including ours, gives an estimate.

Your ATAR in one idea

If you take one idea from this: the VCE gives you scores, and VTAC turns four-and-a-bit of them into a rank. Everything else is detail.

The balance of coursework and exams

The two halves are separate: VCAA sets your study scores, VTAC scales them and calculates the ATAR. Different bodies, different jobs.

What this means for choosing subjects

The takeaway: protect your Primary Four, keep an English in the calculation, and treat the fifth and sixth as the 10% they are.

Common questions

How are VCE results converted to an ATAR?

VTAC scales your VCE study scores to account for each subject’s cohort strength, combines them into an aggregate, and ranks that against your age group to give an ATAR from 0.00 to 99.95.

What is scaling in the VCE?

Scaling adjusts each subject so the same result means the same thing across subjects, based on how strong the group taking it was. It decides how much each of your results contributes to your ATAR.

How many subjects count for the VCE ATAR?

Your aggregate uses your English study score, your best three other study scores, and 10% of your fifth and sixth study scores. So most students complete five or six VCE studies.

Why isn't my ATAR the average of my marks?

Because the ATAR is a rank, not an average. Your scaled results are combined and ranked against your age group, so your ATAR reflects your position, not the arithmetic mean of your results.
