ATAR calculators — common questions

How is the ATAR calculated?

Your senior subject results are scaled so subjects compare fairly, then your best results are combined into an aggregate and ranked against your whole state cohort. The result is the ATAR — a number from 30.00 to 99.95 that shows your rank, not your mark.

Is the ATAR the same in every state?

The ATAR itself is national — a 90 means the top 10% anywhere. But each state calculates it from a different system (HSC in NSW, VCE in Victoria, QCE in Queensland, and so on) through its own admissions centre. That's why there's a separate calculator for each state.

What's a good ATAR?

An ATAR of 90 puts you in the top 10% of your cohort, 95 in the top 5%, and 99+ in the top 1%. But 'good' depends on your course — many degrees accept 70–80, while the most competitive need 95+. The ATAR is a means to an end, not the goal.

What's the highest ATAR you can get?

99.95. It isn't 100 because the ATAR is a rank, not a percentage — 99.95 means the top 0.05% of your state cohort. The lowest reported ATAR is 30.00; below that it's shown as '30.00 or less'.

Which ATAR calculator should I use?

Use the one for your state, since each state's system and scaling differ. NSW students should use the NSW (HSC) calculator, Victorian students the VIC (VCE) one, and so on. The all-states list above links to each.

How accurate are these ATAR calculators?

They're estimates. They model how your marks scale and convert to an ATAR using published scaling patterns, but the official ATAR depends on the exact cohort each year and your admissions centre's full data, which isn't released publicly. Treat them as a guide and a target-setting tool, not a guarantee.
