How your SACE results become an ATAR

South Australia splits the work between two bodies. The SACE Board runs your subjects and reports your results. SATAC scales those results and produces the ATAR. The SACE certificate and the ATAR are separate achievements, and the rules for each are different.

Stage 1 — where a subject result comes from

Your Stage 2 subjects are measured in credits, and the ATAR calculation is built around 20-credit subjects. You need 90 credits of study at Stage 2, which is the requirement most worth confirming early, because the aggregate is assembled from what sits inside that load.

Stage 2 — what SATAC does with it

Scaled result = your subject result, adjusted for the strength of that subject's cohort Aggregate = your best 3 scaled results in full, plus half of a scaled 4th ATAR = your aggregate (out of 90) converted to a rank (0 to 99.95) SATAC counts your best three 20-credit subjects in full, plus half of a scaled fourth (the "0.5" rule), building an aggregate out of 90. You need 90 credits of study at Stage 2.

Why the fourth subject is worth exactly half

This is the rule that catches people out. Your fourth subject does not count like the first three. Half of its scaled result is added, so a strong fourth contributes real value but never as much as a third. Three subjects in full plus half of a fourth is where the maximum of 90 comes from, and it is why South Australia's aggregate looks unlike any other state's.

Worked example

  1. Collect results. Four or more 20-credit Stage 2 subjects, each producing a result.
  2. Scale each one. SATAC adjusts every result for the strength of that subject's cohort.
  3. Take the best three in full. The three highest scaled results are counted completely.
  4. Add half of the fourth. Half of the next-best scaled result is added on top.
  5. Convert to a rank. The aggregate, out of 90, is placed against the state cohort to give the ATAR.

What this calculator does with your SACE results

You enter a result for each subject. The calculator scales each one, counts your best three in full, applies the half-weight rule to your fourth, sums the aggregate out of 90, and converts it to a rank. That is SATAC's sequence, run on your numbers.

The 0.5 rule is applied explicitly, which matters: counting a fourth subject in full is the single most common way a hand-made South Australian estimate ends up far too high.

What it cannot do

Scaling is recalculated each year against the cohort that actually sat each subject, so this year's scaling cannot be known yet. The estimate uses the most recent published data and is best read with a point or two of margin.

The SACE Board sets your subject grades and SATAC issues the official SACE ATAR after scaling.

What throws a SACE estimate off

  • Counting the fourth subject in full. It contributes half. This single error inflates a South Australian estimate more than any other.
  • Assuming a fifth subject adds something. The aggregate is three in full plus half of a fourth. A fifth can only take the place of a weaker result.
  • Overlooking the 90-credit requirement. You need 90 credits of study at Stage 2. Strong results in too few credits do not produce an ATAR.
  • Comparing an aggregate out of 90 to another state's. South Australia's scale is its own. An aggregate of 70 is not weak because other states quote hundreds.
  • Reading the last decimal seriously. The scaling that will apply to your cohort has not been calculated yet.

SACE results and the ATAR — common questions

Is the SACE the same as the ATAR?

No. The SACE is the certificate showing you met South Australia's senior schooling requirements. The ATAR is a separate rank used for tertiary entry, produced by SATAC from your scaled results.

How many subjects count towards a South Australian ATAR?

Effectively three and a half. Your best three 20-credit subjects count in full, and half of a scaled fourth is added on top.

What is the 0.5 rule?

It is the rule that your fourth subject counts at half weight. Half of its scaled result is added to your best three, which is where the maximum aggregate of 90 comes from.

Why is the aggregate out of 90?

Three scaled results counting in full, plus half of a fourth, build to a maximum of 90. That total is what SATAC converts into your ATAR rank.

How many credits do I need at Stage 2?

You need 90 credits of study at Stage 2. It is a requirement for the ATAR, separate from how well any individual subject scales.

Does a fifth subject help my ATAR?

Not by adding to the total, since only three and a half count. It works as insurance: a strong fifth can displace a weaker result from your top three or your half-weighted fourth.

What is the difference between my subject result and my scaled result?

Your subject result is what the SACE Board reports. Your scaled result is that figure adjusted by SATAC for the academic strength of the subject's cohort. Only scaled results enter the aggregate.

Why did two students with the same result get different scaled results?

Because they took different subjects. Scaling reflects the strength of each subject's cohort rather than how difficult the subject felt.
