How your WACE marks become an ATAR

Western Australia divides the work between two bodies. SCSA runs your courses and reports your marks. TISC turns them into an ATAR. Completing the WACE and earning an ATAR are separate achievements, and only some courses count towards the second.

Stage 1 — where a course mark comes from

Each WACE ATAR course gives you a combined mark, built from your school assessment and your written exam. General courses do not produce a mark that feeds an ATAR, which is the first thing to check when you are counting your subjects.

Stage 2 — what TISC does with it

Scaled mark = your course mark (out of 100), scaled by TISC TEA = your best 4 scaled marks, plus bonuses (Methods, Specialist, LOTE) ATAR = your TEA (out of 430) converted to a rank (0 to 99.95) TISC counts your best four scaled courses. Unlimited-potential bonuses apply: an extra 10% of your scaled score for Mathematics Methods, Mathematics Specialist, and Languages (LOTE) is added on top.

Why the TEA runs to 430, not 400

Four scaled marks counting in full would cap the total at 400. The extra 30 comes from the bonuses. Ten per cent of your scaled score in Mathematics Methods, Mathematics Specialist, or a language is added on top of the four, which is why those courses occupy a distinctive place in WA and why the maximum sits above a plain sum of four.

Worked example

  1. Collect marks. Five ATAR courses, each with a combined mark out of 100.
  2. Scale each one. TISC adjusts every mark for the strength of that course's cohort.
  3. Keep the best four. The four highest scaled marks are retained; the fifth drops out of the base total.
  4. Add any bonuses. If you took Methods, Specialist, or a LOTE, 10% of that scaled score is added on top.
  5. Convert to a rank. The TEA, out of 430, is ranked against the state to give the ATAR.

What this calculator does with your WACE marks

You enter a mark for each ATAR course. The calculator scales each one, keeps your best four, applies the Mathematics and LOTE bonuses where they are earned, sums the TEA, and converts it to a rank. That is TISC's sequence, run on your numbers.

The bonuses are handled explicitly, because they are the part of the WA system most often left out of a hand-made estimate — and leaving them out understates the result for anyone taking Methods, Specialist, or a language.

What it cannot do

Scaling is recalculated each year against the cohort that actually sat each course, so this year's scaling cannot be known in advance. The estimate uses the most recent published data and should be read with a point or two of margin.

What throws a WACE estimate off

  • Counting General courses towards the ATAR. Only ATAR courses feed the TEA. Including a General course is the fastest way to an estimate that will not arrive.
  • Forgetting the bonuses. If you take Mathematics Methods, Mathematics Specialist, or a language, 10% of that scaled score is added on top of your best four. Omitting it understates your TEA.
  • Assuming a fifth course adds to the base. Only your best four scaled marks form the base total. A fifth course is insurance, not an addition.
  • Confusing the WACE with the ATAR. The WACE is the certificate showing you met WA's senior schooling requirements. The ATAR is a separate rank for tertiary entry.
  • Reading the last decimal seriously. The scaling that will apply to your cohort has not been calculated yet.

The WACE and your ATAR explained

The WACE is Western Australia's Certificate of Education, awarded when you meet the state's senior schooling requirements. Your ATAR is a separate rank, calculated by TISC from your best four scaled scores, plus the 10 per cent language or mathematics bonus where it applies.

To have an ATAR, your courses need to be ATAR courses, and your scaled scores reflect how you performed against others in each course. Meeting WACE requirements and earning a competitive ATAR are related but distinct: one is about completing your certificate, the other about your rank for university entry.

Because scaling adjusts for the strength of each course's cohort, the same raw mark can contribute differently depending on the course. The calculator above estimates your ATAR from your scores, while TISC produces the official figure each year.

ATAR courses and General courses in the WACE

Not every WACE course counts towards an ATAR. To receive an ATAR in Western Australia, you need results in ATAR courses, which are examined and scaled. General courses and some VET pathways count towards your WACE but do not directly produce an ATAR.

This means your subject choices shape whether, and how, you get an ATAR. Students aiming for university entry usually take a set of ATAR courses and work towards strong scaled scores, plus the language or mathematics bonus where it applies.

The calculator above estimates your ATAR from your scores. When planning your courses, it helps to confirm which are ATAR courses, since only those feed directly into the rank that universities use.

WACE marks and the ATAR — common questions

Is the WACE the same as the ATAR?

No. The WACE is the certificate confirming you met Western Australia's senior schooling requirements. The ATAR is a separate rank used for tertiary entry, and only ATAR courses contribute to it.

Do General courses count towards my ATAR?

No. Only ATAR courses produce the marks TISC uses. General courses can count towards your WACE, but they do not feed the TEA.

How many courses count towards a WA ATAR?

Four. TISC uses your best four scaled marks to build the base of your TEA, then adds any bonuses on top.

What is a TEA in Western Australia?

Your TEA is your best four scaled marks plus any Mathematics or LOTE bonuses, out of a maximum of 430. It is the total that gets converted into your ATAR.

Why is the maximum TEA 430 and not 400?

Four scaled marks counting in full reach 400. The remaining 30 comes from the bonuses: an extra 10% of your scaled score in Mathematics Methods, Mathematics Specialist, or a language is added on top.

How do the Mathematics and LOTE bonuses work?

If you take Mathematics Methods, Mathematics Specialist, or a language, 10% of your scaled score in that course is added to your TEA on top of your best four.

Does a fifth ATAR course help?

It cannot add to the base, since only four count. It works as insurance: if it scales better than one of your top four, it takes that place.

What is the difference between my course mark and my scaled mark?

Your course mark is the combined school assessment and exam mark SCSA reports. Your scaled mark is what TISC produces after adjusting for the strength of that course's cohort. Only scaled marks build the TEA.
