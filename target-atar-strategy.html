Target ATAR strategy

The most effective way to aim at an ATAR is to work backwards from where you want to end up. Here is how to set a target ATAR, find the marks that reach it, and build a plan around your goal.

What a target ATAR strategy is

A target ATAR strategy flips the usual approach. Instead of working forwards from your current marks and hoping for the best, you start from where you want to end up and work backwards to what you need to do.

The goal is a clear, concrete target: the ATAR your chosen course requires, and the subject marks that would get you there. That turns a vague hope into a specific plan you can act on.

This backwards approach is powerful because it makes your effort purposeful. You know exactly what you are aiming for and roughly what it takes, which is far more motivating than studying without a target.

Working backwards from your goal

Working backwards means starting with the end and reasoning back to the present. Begin with the course you want, find its ATAR cutoff, then figure out the marks across your subjects that would produce that ATAR.

From there, you compare those required marks to your current ones. The gap between them is your task: the improvement you need, subject by subject, to reach your target.

This gives you a map. Rather than a vague sense of “do well”, you have a specific destination and a route to it, which makes planning your effort straightforward.

Start with the cutoff

The first step is to find the ATAR cutoff for the course you want. Cutoffs are published by universities and admissions centres, and they indicate the ATAR needed for entry, though they shift a little year to year.

Note that cutoffs are indicative, not fixed guarantees. They can move with demand, and some courses consider more than ATAR alone. So treat the cutoff as a target to clear comfortably, not a line to just scrape.

If you are considering several courses, use the highest cutoff among them as your target. Aiming for the toughest keeps all your options open.

Find the marks you need

Once you have a target ATAR, work out the subject marks that reach it. Because scaling varies by subject, the marks needed differ: a lower mark in a strong-scaling subject can match a higher mark in a broad one.

A calculator makes this concrete. Enter a target and adjust subject marks to see what combination reaches your ATAR, so you can see realistic mark targets for each of your actual subjects.

Our ATAR predictor lets you test combinations, and the scaling calculator shows how each subject scales, so your mark targets reflect real scaling rather than guesswork.

Setting a realistic target

A good target stretches you without being fantasy. Base it on your current position: look at your recent marks and estimated ATAR, then set a target that is ambitious but reachable with focused effort.

If your goal course sits far above your current estimate, be honest about the gap and the time you have. A large gap is not impossible, but it needs a serious plan and realistic expectations. A modest gap is very achievable.

It also helps to have a backup. Aim high, and identify a solid alternative course with a lower cutoff, so you have a plan whether or not you hit your top target.

What scores give a 90+ ATAR?

Students often ask what marks produce a 90 or higher ATAR, but there is no single answer, because it depends on your subjects and their scaling. Strong marks in well-scaling subjects reach a high ATAR with lower raw marks than broad subjects would.

As a rough sense, a 90+ ATAR generally requires you to rank near the top of a strong set of subjects, with high raw marks that scale well, or very high marks in more moderate ones. The exact combination varies by student.

So rather than chase a fixed mark, use a calculator with your own subjects to see what a 90+ needs for you. That gives a real target, not a one-size-fits-all figure that may not fit your subjects.

Planning around your target

With target marks for each subject, you can plan your effort. Compare your target marks to your current ones, and direct the most effort at the subjects with the biggest gaps to close.

This keeps your work focused on what actually reaches your goal. A subject already at its target needs maintenance; one well below needs attention. The plan writes itself once you know the gaps. See how to improve your predicted ATAR.

Tracking against your target

A target is only useful if you track against it. Re-check your estimated ATAR as your marks update, and compare it to your target. That tells you whether you are on course or need to adjust.

Regular tracking keeps your goal alive and your effort honest. If you are ahead, you can consolidate; if behind, you can refocus, while there is still time to act.

Aim a little above the cutoff

Because cutoffs shift and predictions are estimates, aim a little above your target cutoff rather than exactly at it. A small buffer protects you against a cutoff rising or your result landing slightly below expectation.

Aiming above the line also gives you options: a higher ATAR opens more courses and more flexibility. So set your working target a few points above the cutoff you need, and treat clearing it comfortably as the goal.

Build your target plan

To build your plan, start with our ATAR predictor. Set your target ATAR, test subject-mark combinations that reach it, and compare them to your current marks to find your gaps.

Then direct your effort at the biggest gaps, aim a little above your cutoff for safety, and re-check as you go. A clear target, tracked over time, turns a vague worry into a concrete, achievable plan.

Why a target beats hoping

Studying without a target is like driving without a destination: you move, but with no way to judge progress. A target ATAR gives your effort direction and a benchmark, so you always know whether you are on course.

A clear target also sustains motivation. It is far easier to push through a hard revision session when you know exactly what it is for, and how close it brings you to a course you want. Purpose makes effort easier to find.

So the act of setting a target is valuable in itself, before any study happens. It converts a vague hope into a concrete goal you can plan around and measure against.

Reviewing your target over time

Your target is not set in stone. As you progress, review it against your actual marks. If you are consistently ahead, you might raise your sights to a more competitive course. If you are behind, you can refocus or firm up a backup.

Regular review keeps your target realistic and your plan responsive. The aim is a goal that stays ambitious but achievable as your position becomes clearer through Year 12, so you finish aimed at the best course genuinely within reach.
