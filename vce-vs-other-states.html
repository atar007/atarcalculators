VCE vs other state systems

Students often ask whether the VCE is easier or harder than the HSC, VCE or other systems. Here is the honest answer, and why the question matters less than it seems.

By the ATARCalculators Editorial Team ·Reviewed 4 July 2026 ·Updated July 2026 ·4 min read

Key takeaways

  • The VCE is not inherently easier or harder than other systems.
  • The ATAR is a national rank — a given ATAR means the same everywhere.
  • State median differences mostly reflect participation, not difficulty.
  • Each system assesses differently, but all produce the same ATAR.
  • Scaling equalises subjects within each state.
  • You cannot get an easier ATAR by moving states.

The ATAR is national

The ATAR is a single national rank. An 85.00 earned in Victoria means the same as an 85.00 earned in Perth: you finished ahead of roughly 85% of your state's Year 12 age group.

Interstate universities read it without translation. The rank arrives already on a common scale.

How the systems differ

Victoria's route to that rank is its own. Your best four scaled study scores count in full, then 10% of your fifth and sixth are added, building an aggregate of roughly 210. New South Wales and Queensland work to 500; South Australia to 90.

Those totals cannot be compared. Each is calibrated only against its own state's cohort, so an aggregate of 180 in Victoria and 400 in NSW say nothing about each other.

What is unique about the VCE

All VCE students sit the General Achievement Test, or GAT. It does not directly count towards your study scores. Instead, VCAA uses it to check and moderate assessments across the state, so it is worth taking seriously even though it is not scored like a subject.

No other state runs an equivalent statewide moderation instrument sat by every student. It is the most distinctive feature of the Victorian system.

Why state medians differ

Medians differ between states because participation differs. A state where more students receive an ATAR reports a lower median; a state where fewer do reports a higher one.

That is arithmetic about who took part, not evidence about standards. Victoria's median describes Victoria's cohort and nothing else.

Is any state easier?

No. Your ATAR positions you inside your own state's cohort, so "easier" would require a weaker cohort rather than gentler assessment.

Victoria's increments for a fifth and sixth study are unusual, but they are small — a tenth each. They change the shape of the aggregate, not its difficulty.

How scaling fits in

Every state scales so that different subjects can be compared before ranking. VTAC does it for Victoria, and publishes the outcome more openly than most.

That openness is often misread. A published Victorian scaling report describes Victorian cohorts, and cannot be laid alongside another state's figures.

Does moving states help your ATAR?

No. Moving states exchanges one rulebook and cohort for another without improving your position in either.

The disruption is concrete: Victoria's Unit 3&4 structure, its SACs, and the GAT do not have direct equivalents elsewhere. A mid-stream move costs continuity and gains nothing.

Interstate students and the ATAR

A Victorian ATAR is read at face value across the country. No university converts or discounts it.

What differs interstate is prerequisites and adjustment schemes, which each university sets for itself.

What to focus on instead

Focus on your Primary Four — the studies that count in full. That is where the aggregate is decided, and it is the one part of the system fully within your control.

Time spent comparing Victoria to New South Wales is time spent on numbers that were never comparable.

The bottom line

The VCE reaches the national rank via study scores out of 50, four counted in full, two increments, and a GAT that quietly moderates the lot. It is a distinctive system, not an easier or harder one.

A closer look at each system

Each state builds its certificate its own way. Victoria takes four scaled study scores in full plus a tenth of two more, totalling around 210. New South Wales counts ten units to 500. Tasmania adds five scores to 75. South Australia counts three and a half to 90.

Four different methods, one shared national rank.

The myth of the easy state

The easy-state idea is stubborn and wrong. The ATAR ranks you against your own state's age group, and VTAC has already equalised studies through scaling before that ranking happens.

For another state to be easier, its cohort would need to be weaker than Victoria's — not its assessment softer.

What actually varies between states

What varies is the experience of the two years. Victoria runs SACs through the year, sits every student through the GAT, and publishes its scaling report openly.

Those change how your VCE feels. They do not change whether it is fair.

What to take away

State comparison is a good argument and a bad plan. The VCE reaches the national rank via study scores out of 50, four counted fully, two increments, and a GAT quietly holding the whole thing steady.

Focus on your own rank

Since no state issues easier ATARs, the question worth asking is how you rank inside your Primary Four. Those are the studies that count in full, and they decide your aggregate.

Common questions

Is the VCE harder than the HSC or VCE?

Neither is inherently harder. The ATAR is a national rank, so a given ATAR means the same across states. The systems assess students differently, but all produce the same national ATAR.

Are ATARs comparable across states?

Yes. The ATAR is a national rank on the same 0.00 to 99.95 scale, so a 90 means the same thing wherever you study. That is the whole point of a national rank.

Does one state give higher ATARs?

No state hands out easier ATARs. Within each state, scaling equalises subjects and the ATAR ranks students against their age group, so a given ATAR represents the same percentile everywhere.

Why do state medians differ?

Mostly because of participation rates. In some states a larger share of students receive an ATAR, while in others more take vocational paths, which shifts the median of those who do get one.
