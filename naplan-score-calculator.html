How NAPLAN scoring works

NAPLAN reports each domain on a national numeric scale, then places you in one of four proficiency levels. Here is how a scale score becomes a level.

The formula

Scale score = your result on the national NAPLAN scale for that domain Proficiency level = which of four bands your scale score falls into Levels: Needs additional support, Developing, Strong, Exceeding Since 2023, NAPLAN uses four proficiency levels instead of the old ten-band system. The scale score is the underlying number; the level is the plain-language band it maps to, and the cut-points differ by year level.

Step by step

  1. Get your scale score. Each domain (reading, writing, numeracy, and language conventions) has a scale score on the national NAPLAN scale.
  2. Find the cut-points for the year. Each year level (3, 5, 7 or 9) has its own boundaries between the four proficiency levels.
  3. Match the score to a level. Your scale score is compared to those boundaries to give one of the four levels for that domain.
  4. Read the result. You get the proficiency level for each domain, which is how NAPLAN results are now reported to families.

Worked example

The four proficiency levels, in order, and what they signal:

A result in the Strong or Exceeding level is a good outcome. Because the cut-points differ by year level, the same scale score can fall in different levels in Year 3 versus Year 9. Use the calculator above to see where a scale score lands.

How NAPLAN scores became proficiency levels

Until 2022, NAPLAN reported results on a 10-band numerical scale. Since 2023, every result is reported as one of four proficiency levels: Exceeding, Strong, Developing, or Needs additional support. The scale score still appears on the report, sitting on a common scale of roughly 0 to 1000 that runs from Year 3 to Year 9.

The line between Developing and Strong is the national minimum standard. And it is set higher for each year level. A score of 480 is a Strong result in Year 5 but only Developing in Year 9. This is because more is expected as students get older.

What the levels mean

Exceeding means performing well above the standard for the year level. Strong means at or above the standard, which is the on-track result. Developing means working towards the standard, and is a signal to focus on specific skills. Needs additional support means well below the standard, and usually means targeted help at school and home.

Use it as a starting point

NAPLAN is a snapshot, not a verdict. A result below Strong is diagnostic information, not a failure. The best next step is to find which specific skills are below standard and work on those. To see how this maps to the old bands, use the NAPLAN band calculator. Year 9 students in NSW should also read the Year 9 NAPLAN calculator for more.

NAPLAN is run by ACARA, and official results and the proficiency levels are published at nap.edu.au.

Common mistakes reading NAPLAN scores

These are the misunderstandings families most often have about NAPLAN results.

  • Comparing to the old bands. NAPLAN moved from ten bands to four proficiency levels in 2023. Old band numbers do not map neatly onto the new levels.
  • Assuming the same score means the same level every year. The boundaries between levels differ by year level, so the same scale score can sit in different levels.
  • Treating NAPLAN as a selective test. NAPLAN is a national check of literacy and numeracy, not the selective-school or OC test, though the skills overlap.
  • Reading one domain as the whole picture. Reading, writing, numeracy and language conventions are separate. A strong result in one does not describe the others.
  • Over-weighting a single test. NAPLAN is one snapshot on one set of days. It is useful, but it does not define a student.

NAPLAN scores — common questions

How are NAPLAN proficiency levels worked out?

Your scale score is compared to the cut scores ACARA sets for your year level and domain. Above the national minimum standard is Strong, well above is Exceeding, below is Developing or Needs additional support.

What is a good NAPLAN score?

Strong or Exceeding means your child is at or above the national standard for their year level. Around two thirds of students reach Strong or Exceeding in reading and numeracy.

Why is the same score Strong in one year and Developing in another?

Because the standard rises with each year level. The cut score for Strong in Year 9 is much higher than in Year 5, since older students are expected to do more.

Is this calculator exact?

No, it uses indicative thresholds for Reading and Numeracy. The exact cut scores are set each year and printed on your child's official report. Always use that report for the definitive result.

Why are Developing and Needs additional support shown together?

ACARA does not publish the boundary between those two lower levels as a single national figure, so this tool groups them. Your child's report shows which of the two applies.

Does a low NAPLAN score affect my child's future?

No. NAPLAN does not decide promotion, school placement or career outcomes. One thing to note in NSW. The HSC minimum standard is a separate requirement, met by online tests in Years 10 to 12, and Year 9 NAPLAN does not count toward it.
