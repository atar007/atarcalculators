How to improve your QLD ATAR

There is still plenty you can do in Year 12 to lift your ATAR. This guide covers the levers that actually work in the QCE, from internal assessments to bonus points.

By the ATARCalculators Editorial Team ·Reviewed 4 July 2026 ·Updated July 2026 ·4 min read

Focus on your internal assessments

In the QCE, most of your result in a General subject comes from internal assessments across the year. These are set at your school and confirmed by QCAA.

This is good news: it means the work you do now counts, long before the external exam. Each internal task is a chance to build a strong result, so treat every one as if it matters.

Prepare for the external exam

Each General subject also has an external exam, written and marked by QCAA. For most subjects it is worth about a quarter of your result. For maths and science subjects it is worth half.

So exam preparation matters, especially in maths and science. Past papers under timed conditions are the most reliable way to get ready.

Prioritise the subjects you can score highly in

Your ATAR uses your best five scaled results, so your strongest subjects do the heavy lifting. It makes sense to invest most in the subjects where you can score near the top.

This does not mean neglecting others, but it does mean being strategic. An hour spent lifting a strong subject often pays off more than an hour rescuing a weak one.

Use scaling wisely, not blindly

Understanding scaling helps, but do not let it drive panic. A high-scaling subject only helps if you score well in it. A strong result in a subject you are good at beats a weak result in a high-scaling one.

Choose subjects you can excel in first, and let scaling be a tiebreaker. See best scaling subjects in QLD.

Check for university bonus points

Many Queensland universities add adjustment factors, or bonus points, to your rank. You might qualify based on where you live, subjects you take, or difficulty you faced during Year 12.

These lift your selection rank above your ATAR for that course, sometimes by several points. It is worth checking early, because some schemes need an application. See how bonus points work.

Keep a safety subject

Because only your best five results count, a spare subject is a safety net. If one subject goes poorly, your top five still carry your ATAR and the weak one drops out.

A subject you enjoy and can do reasonably well in makes a good safety choice. It protects your ATAR without adding much stress.

Habits that lift your results

The unglamorous habits work best: consistent study, past papers under timed conditions, and acting on feedback from marked tasks. Results are won across the year, not in a final sprint.

Look after yourself too. Sleep, breaks and steady routines keep you performing across every assessment, which is exactly what a strong result needs.

Track your QLD ATAR

Seeing your progress helps you focus. Our QLD ATAR calculator estimates your ATAR from your current results, so you can watch it move as your results improve.

Re-run it after each round of assessments. A rising estimate is motivating, and it tells you where your effort is paying off.

Doing well in internal assessment

In Queensland, most of your result in a General subject comes from three internal assessments across the year. These are marked at your school against QCAA’s standards and confirmed by QCAA.

This is good news: it means the work you do now counts, long before the external exam. Treat each internal task as if it matters, because together they make up the bulk of your subject result.

Preparing for the external exam

Each General subject ends with an external exam, worth about a quarter of your result, or half in maths and science. So exam preparation matters, especially in those subjects.

Past papers under timed conditions are the most reliable way to prepare. Review where you lost marks rather than just repeating papers, so your practice targets your weak areas.

Choosing subjects you can score in

In Queensland, General subjects are the ones that count towards your ATAR, so your choice among them matters. Picking subjects that genuinely suit your strengths, rather than ones that simply sound impressive, tends to produce better scaled results.

It also helps to think ahead to the courses you are considering. Some university degrees expect specific subjects as prerequisites, so checking those early means your strong results also open the doors you actually want.

Adjustments and EAS in QLD

Many Queensland universities add adjustments to your rank, and QTAC runs the Educational Access Scheme, or EAS, for students who faced disadvantage during Year 12.

These lift your selection rank above your ATAR for a course, sometimes by several points. They are worth checking early, because EAS and some schemes need an application. See how selection rank works.

A term-by-term plan

Spread your effort across the year. Early on, build strong habits and aim to do well in each internal assessment. Through the middle of the year, keep that consistency and start timed past-paper practice.

In the final stretch, focus on exam technique and your weak areas, and look after your health so you perform when it counts. Consistency across the year beats a last-minute sprint every time.

Looking after your wellbeing

Your results depend on being able to perform, and that needs a functioning you. Sleep, regular breaks, exercise and time away from study are not distractions from your ATAR — they protect it.

Burnout costs marks. A steady routine that you can sustain across the year will beat an intense sprint that leaves you exhausted before the exams.

Study habits that lift internal marks

The habits that lift internal marks are unglamorous but reliable. Consistent study beats last-minute cramming. Timed practice builds exam stamina. Spacing your revision over weeks helps it stick.

Because most of your subject result is internal assessment, these habits pay off across the whole year, not just at exam time. Small, steady effort compounds into a stronger result.

Getting feedback and acting on it

Marked assessments are a goldmine, if you use them. Every returned task shows you exactly where you lost marks. Acting on that feedback is one of the fastest ways to improve.

Ask your teachers what would lift your result, and be specific. A targeted fix in one area often lifts your next task more than hours of unfocused study.

Start where you are

Whatever your results look like now, the useful question is what you can do next, not what you wish you had done. Your result can still move, and most of your subject result comes from internal work you are building right now.

Pick one subject and one habit to improve this week, and act on it. Momentum in Year 12 comes from small starts, and results that are climbing are exactly what a strong ATAR needs.

A note on subject selection

Some of the biggest gains come before Year 12, when you choose your subjects. Picking subjects you can do well in, that keep your course options open, sets you up for a strong result.

If you are still choosing, weigh your strengths, your interest, and any prerequisites for courses you might want. A good subject mix makes the whole of Year 12 easier to perform in.

Small gains add up

You do not need a dramatic turnaround to lift your ATAR. Small, steady gains — a better internal task here, a few more marks in an exam there — compound across your best five results into a meaningful difference.

So focus on the next assessment, not the whole year at once. Consistent, modest improvement, task after task, is exactly what builds a stronger result by the time the exams arrive.

Common questions

Can you still improve your ATAR in Year 12?

Yes. Most of your General subject result comes from internal assessments across the year, so strong, consistent results still lift your ATAR. Preparing well for the external exam matters too.

Does subject choice affect your ATAR?

Yes, but not the way many think. Your ATAR uses your best five results, so the subjects you score highly in matter most. A strong result in a subject you are good at beats a weak one in a high-scaling subject.

Do adjustment or bonus points raise your ATAR?

They do not change your ATAR itself, but they raise your selection rank for a course, which is what universities use for entry. Many Queensland universities offer them based on location, subjects, or difficulty faced.

How important are your internal assessments?

Very. Internal assessments make up most of your result in each General subject, with the external exam worth about a quarter for most subjects and half for maths and science. Strong internal results lift your ATAR.

Does internal assessment count towards my QLD ATAR?

Yes. Most of your result in a General subject comes from three internal assessments across the year, marked against QCAA standards. Strong, consistent internal work builds a good subject result.

What is EAS and can it help my ATAR?

EAS, the Educational Access Scheme run by QTAC, supports students who faced disadvantage during Year 12. It can lift your selection rank for a course above your ATAR, and it needs an application, so check early.
