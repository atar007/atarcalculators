How to improve your SA ATAR: a Year 12 game plan

You have more control over your ATAR than it feels. This guide covers the levers that actually move it in the SACE system, and where students waste their time.

By the ATARCalculators Editorial Team ·Reviewed 3 July 2026 ·Updated July 2026 ·9 min read

To improve your SA ATAR, focus on your ranking within each subject, because that drives scaling. Protect your best three scaled scores, since they carry the most weight. Master your exams and assessments, choose subjects you can score highly in, and check which bonus points you qualify for. Small, steady gains across your best subjects add up to a higher rank.

Key takeaways

  • Your rank within each subject drives scaling, so aim to climb it.
  • Your best three scaled scores carry the most weight, so protect them.
  • Choose subjects you can score highly in, not just ones that scale.
  • Strong Stage 2 assessments and exams both matter.
  • Bonus points can lift your selection rank above your raw ATAR.
  • Consistent work beats last-minute cramming in a scaled system.

Focus on your ranking within each subject

In a scaled system, your position inside each subject is what carries through. Climbing your subject ranking is the only lever that reliably moves your ATAR — and in South Australia, where three subjects carry your aggregate, it moves it hard.

Protect your best three scores

Your best three scaled results form the core of your aggregate, with half of a fourth added on top, out of 90. No other state concentrates so much into so few subjects — as our SA ATAR guide sets out in full.

That makes your top three worth defending above everything else. A point gained there is worth more than a point gained anywhere else in the country.

Master your exams and assessments

SACE results draw on your assessments across Stage 2 and your exams. Both feed the result SATAC scales, so neglecting either half throws away part of the mark before scaling even begins.

Choose subjects you can do well in

Subject choice matters, though not the way most students assume. A high-scaling subject you struggle in still produces a weak result — and with only three and a half counting, one weak result is genuinely expensive.

Complete your SACE requirements

To get an ATAR you must complete your SACE, and that includes the compulsory Research Project alongside the literacy and numeracy requirements. South Australia is the only state that asks for the Research Project.

It is worth tracking deliberately, because a missed requirement stops the ATAR regardless of how well your subjects went.

Use adjustment and bonus points

Bonus points do not touch your ATAR. They adjust your selection rank for particular courses at particular universities, which is a different number arriving at a later stage.

Study habits that move the needle

Not all study returns the same value. Spacing revision, testing yourself rather than rereading, and working problems under time pressure all outperform passive review — and with three subjects carrying your aggregate, targeting that effort is straightforward.

Build a study timetable that works

A timetable converts intention into hours. Keep it simple enough that you actually follow it, and weight it towards the three subjects most likely to form your counted results.

Handle exam stress

Some nerves sharpen you; sustained stress costs marks and sleep. Protect the sleep, keep some structure outside study, and treat the pressure as manageable rather than as evidence about your ability.

Track your progress

You improve what you measure. Keep a simple record of your marks and, where you can find it, your rank inside each subject. In a three-subject system, knowing which subject is slipping is half the battle.

Common questions

Can you still improve your ATAR in Year 12?

Yes. Because your ATAR reflects your ranking within each subject, steady improvement in your assessments and exams can lift your scaled scores and your final ATAR.

What matters most for the SA ATAR?

Your best three scaled scores carry the most weight in your aggregate. So protecting and lifting your three strongest subjects is one of the most effective things you can do.

Does subject choice affect your ATAR?

Yes, but mainly through how well you score. Choose subjects you can perform strongly in, because those become your best three. A high-scaling subject only helps if you rank well within its group.

Do bonus points raise your ATAR?

No, they raise your selection rank, not your ATAR itself. That rank is what universities use for offers, so bonus points can still bring a course within reach.

How many subjects should I take for the ATAR?

Enough Stage 2 Tertiary Admission Subjects to cover your best three plus a flexible option, usually four or five. This gives a safety margin if one subject goes poorly.

What is the fastest way to lift my ATAR?

There is no single trick, but improving your rank in each subject, especially your best three, is the most reliable lever. Do that through steady assessments, strong exam skills and smart subject choice.

Do I need to complete SACE to get an ATAR?

Yes. You must complete your SACE, including requirements like the Research Project, to receive an ATAR. Keep track of what you still need, so a missed requirement never blocks your result.

How do I know if I'm on track?

Track your marks and where you sit in each subject, then estimate your ATAR as you go. If you are below your target, you still have time to adjust your study and subject focus.
