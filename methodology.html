What is the ATAR?

The Australian Tertiary Admission Rank (ATAR) is the primary tool universities use to compare Year 12 school leavers across the country. It's a rank, not a score — it tells you what percentage of the Year 12 cohort you outperformed.

The scale runs from 0.00 to 99.95 in increments of 0.05 (ATARs below 30.00 are reported as “30.00 or less”):

  • An ATAR of 99.95 means you're in the top 0.05% of the entire Year 12 cohort.
  • An ATAR of 90.00 means you outperformed roughly 90% of the cohort.
  • An ATAR of 50.00 means you finished ahead of about half of your age group. Because roughly a third of that age group does not receive an ATAR, the median ATAR among students who do is closer to 70.

The ATAR is not calculated from your raw marks directly. Each state runs Year 12 differently (HSC, VCE, QCE, etc.). But the ATAR is the universal output that all states feed into for university admissions.

The 4-step process

Whichever state's calculator you use, the underlying flow is the same:

  1. Collect your marks — Type your raw marks for each subject (predicted, internal, or trial — whatever you have).
  2. Scale them — Each subject is scaled using the latest official scaling report. Harder subjects get scaled up; easier subjects get scaled down.
  3. Aggregate — Your scaled marks are combined into an aggregate score, following your state's specific counting rules.
  4. Convert to rank — Your aggregate is compared against past cohorts and converted into an ATAR estimate with a confidence range.
Why we show a range, not a single number. Your aggregate maps to a slightly different ATAR each year based on the cohort. Showing "92.45 ± 1.20" is more honest than a single false-precision number.

State-specific rules

Each state counts subjects differently to form your aggregate. The full breakdown:

NSW — HSC (UAC)

  • Counts the best 10 units of scaled marks
  • At least 2 units of English are mandatory (HSC requirement)
  • If you do both Mathematics Advanced and Mathematics Extension 1, only the higher-scaling combination counts
  • Aggregate is out of 500 (10 units × 50 max scaled marks per unit)

VIC — VCE (VTAC)

  • Counts the top 4 study scores at full weight, plus 10% of the next 2
  • One of the top 4 must be an English subject (English, Literature, EAL, or English Language)
  • VTAC primary 4 = 1 English + 3 best other subjects
  • Aggregate is out of 210

QLD — QCE (QTAC)

  • Counts the best 5 results from General subjects, plus one Applied subject if it improves the total
  • Subjects scored 0–100 on the Subject Result scale
  • Convert to scaled scores using QTAC's annual scaling tables
  • Aggregate is out of 500

WA — WACE (TISC)

  • Counts the best 4 ATAR-course scaled scores
  • 10% bonus for Mathematics Methods, Mathematics Specialist, and Languages (LOTE)
  • Must have completed at least 4 ATAR courses (one being English)

SA — SACE (SATAC)

  • Counts a maximum of 90 credits at Stage 2
  • Best 3 × 20-credit subjects + Research Project, or alternative combinations
  • Subjects scaled by SATAC

TAS — TCE (TASC / UTAS)

  • Counts best 5 Level 3 or Level 4 subjects across Years 11 and 12 combined
  • UTAS calculates the ATAR using a unique scaled-aggregate model

ACT — BSSS (UAC)

  • Counts top 3 majors + 0.6 × next best major or minor
  • BSSS calculates an Aggregate, then UAC converts to ATAR
  • Includes the AST (ACT Scaling Test) result for cross-college comparison

NT — NTCET (uses SACE)

  • NT students sit the SACE curriculum
  • ATAR calculated by SATAC under SACE rules
  • Same counting structure as SA

How scaling works

Scaling is the most misunderstood part of the ATAR. The short version:

Some subjects are statistically harder than others. A "Band 6" in English Advanced doesn't mean the same thing as a "Band 6" in Standard English. To compare fairly, every state's tertiary body publishes scaling figures based on the actual performance of the cohort.

Top-scaling NSW HSC subjects are consistently the higher mathematics, sciences and languages. Indicative scaled means from a recent UAC scaling report (these shift slightly each year — always check the current report):

  • Mathematics Extension 2 — mean scaled around 43/50
  • Mathematics Extension 1 — mean scaled around 38/50
  • Physics — mean around 32/50
  • Chemistry — mean around 32/50
  • English Extension 2 — mean around 38/50

Lower-scaling subjects: General Mathematics, PDHPE, CAFS, and some Industry & Enterprise courses sit closer to the mid-20s.

Important: "High-scaling" doesn't automatically mean "better for your ATAR." A Band 6 in a normally-scaled subject often beats a Band 4 in Extension Maths. Pick subjects you can perform well in, not just ones that scale highest.

Aggregate & rank

Once your scaled marks are calculated, your aggregate is the sum of your counted scaled marks according to your state's rules.

The aggregate is then compared to past cohorts to produce a percentile rank:

  • NSW aggregate of ~470/500 typically ranks around ATAR 99.3.
  • NSW aggregate of ~430/500 typically ranks around ATAR 97.
  • NSW aggregate of ~370/500 typically ranks around ATAR 90.
  • NSW aggregate of ~290/500 typically ranks around ATAR 77.

These mappings shift slightly each year based on cohort distribution. Our calculators use recent published scaling data to produce a stable estimate.

Update schedule

Scaling reports are released after each year's Year 12 results:

  • UAC (NSW): a preliminary scaling report in December, with a fuller report around the middle of the following year
  • VTAC (VIC): scaling report published in the months after results
  • QTAC (QLD): ATAR report published after results
  • TISC (WA), SATAC (SA & NT), TASC (TAS): published in the months after results
  • BSSS (ACT): released alongside UAC's report

We update our calculators when each new report is published. The bottom of every calculator shows which report version is in use.

Accuracy & verification

How well does our estimator match the official ATAR? We benchmark against:

  • UAC's ATAR Compass — the official NSW estimator. Our NSW methodology matches theirs (same scaling source, same aggregation rules).
  • Past student feedback — students who received their official ATAR can compare it to our estimate.
  • Cohort variance — we account for the fact that each year's cohort is slightly different, which is why we show a confidence range.

If you find a discrepancy, please report it. Real student-result comparisons are how we improve.

Editorial standards & review

This methodology — and every calculator and guide on the site — is maintained by the ATARCalculators Editorial Team, an independent group that works with Australian senior-secondary and tertiary-admissions data. We're not a tutoring brand and not academics-for-hire. Our one job is turning the official reports into tools students can use. Our standards are simple and strict:

  • Sourced, not guessed. No scaling factor, aggregate rule or cutoff is published unless it traces to an official public report listed above. Never to memory or another calculator.
  • Verified against the original. We check every figure against the source report itself, not a third-party summary of it.
  • Reviewed on release. When a new annual report is published, we update the affected calculators and guides and refresh the "last reviewed" date on the page.
  • Corrected openly. Reader-reported errors are verified against the source and fixed promptly, and significant changes are recorded in the changelog below.
  • Independent. No university, tutoring company or admissions body pays for placement or influences our figures. The calculators stay neutral and free.

Every guide carries a "last reviewed" date and the official sources behind it. So you always know how current the information is and where to verify it.

Limitations

What our calculator cannot do:
  • Predict your raw marks. We need your marks as input — we don't estimate what you'll score in your final exams.
  • Account for this year's cohort distribution (it doesn't exist until results come out).
  • Replace the official ATAR. Your real ATAR comes from your state's tertiary admissions body in December/January.
  • Tell you what universities will accept you. Cutoffs change yearly and depend on demand.

Treat our estimate as directionally correct, not exact. If we say you're at ATAR 92, you're likely between 91 and 93 — not exactly 92.45.
