How the NSW selective placement score works

Entry to a NSW selective high school is decided by a placement score built from four equally weighted test sections. Here is exactly how that score is put together.

The formula

Placement score = reading + mathematical reasoning + thinking skills + writing Each of the four sections is scaled and weighted equally (25% each) The total is a placement score out of 120 The four sections carry equal weight, so no single area dominates. Scores are scaled before they are combined, and there is no officially published cut-off; places go to the highest scorers who list a school as a preference.

Step by step

  1. Sit the four sections. The test covers reading, mathematical reasoning, thinking skills, and writing. Each is marked separately.
  2. Each section is scaled. Raw marks are scaled so the four sections compare fairly and combine on the same footing.
  3. The sections are added equally. The four scaled sections are summed, each contributing a quarter, into a placement score out of 120.
  4. Places are offered by rank. Students are ranked by placement score against their school preferences. The strongest schools fill from the very top each year.

How placement works

There are around 4,248 Year 7 selective places across roughly 47 schools, against 14,000 to 15,000 applicants each year. About 20% of places are set aside through the Equity Placement Model; the rest are allocated on rank against the schools a family nominates.

There are no fixed, officially published cut-off scores. A school's effective threshold is simply the score of the lowest-ranked student who accepted a place that year, and it moves with demand. Always confirm the current process with the NSW Department of Education.

The four sections, equally weighted

The test has four sections: Reading, Mathematical Reasoning, Thinking Skills, and Writing. Since the 2025 reforms, each carries 25% of the placement score. Writing used to count for less. So a child who writes poorly under timed conditions is now at a real disadvantage, no matter how strong their other sections are.

Because the sections are equal, the limiting section usually decides the result. A balanced profile across all four beats a spiky one with a single very strong area.

Scaled scores, not raw marks

Results are reported as scaled scores so they can be compared fairly across years of differing difficulty. You cannot read a placement score as a count of correct answers. The official report also shows a percentile band for each section, such as top 10% or next 15%.

Common mistakes with the NSW selective test

These are the misunderstandings families run into most.

  • Assuming there is a fixed cut-off. Cut-offs are not published and change every year with the applicant pool. Any number you see online is an estimate.
  • Thinking one strong section carries you. All four sections are weighted equally. A weak writing score is not rescued by a top maths score.
  • Confusing raw marks with the placement score. Sections are scaled before they are combined. Raw marks do not add up directly to the placement score.
  • Ignoring school preferences. Your score is matched against the schools you list. Listing only the most competitive school can mean missing an offer you would have received elsewhere.
  • Treating an estimate as a guarantee. This tool gives an indicative score. The official result comes from the department after moderation.

NSW selective test — common questions

How is the NSW selective placement score calculated?

In the current model, each of the four sections is scaled and, as widely reported, weighted equally. Those four are Reading, Mathematical Reasoning, Thinking Skills and Writing. Together they combine into a placement score that ranks students statewide. The Department does not publish the exact weightings or a score total. So 25% each and a total of 120 are the common understanding rather than official figures.

What score do I need to get into a selective school?

There is no published cut-off. A school's threshold is the score of the lowest-ranked student who accepted a place that year, and it changes with demand. The most sought-after schools sit at the very top each year.

Is this calculator the official score?

No. It is an indicative planning estimate based on the equal-weighting model. The real test reports scaled scores, and only the NSW Department of Education issues the official placement score.

Does writing really count as much as maths now?

In the current model, Writing is widely reported to count equally with the other three sections. That is a change from the older scheme, where it counted less. So a weak writing response now has a much bigger impact than before. Exact weightings are not published by the Department.

How many selective places are there?

Around 4,248 Year 7 places across roughly 47 selective and partially selective schools, against 14,000 to 15,000 applicants each year.

What are the percentile bands on the report?

For each section the report shows a band such as top 10%, next 15%, next 25%, or lowest 50%. These compare your child to other test-takers, not to a percentage of correct answers.
