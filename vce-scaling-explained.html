VCE scaling explained

Scaling is the most misunderstood part of the VCE. This guide explains what it is, why it exists, and how it decides which subjects help your ATAR most.

VCE scaling is the step where VTAC adjusts each study score before your aggregate is built. Your study score already ranks you inside your own study, out of 50; scaling decides what that position is worth once studies are compared. VTAC then counts your best four scaled scores in full and adds 10% of your fifth and sixth. Scaling follows the strength of a study's cohort, never the difficulty of its content.

  • Victoria counts your best four in full, then adds 10% of your 5th and 6th.
  • A study scales up when its cohort is academically strong, not when its content is hard.

What is VCE scaling?

Scaling is the conversion VTAC applies to each study score so that a 35 in one study is worth the same as a 35 in another. It happens after VCAA reports your scores, on the way to your aggregate.

Victoria's system has a useful quirk: your study score already positions you within your study, on a scale to 50 with 30 sitting around the middle. Scaling does not re-rank you. It decides what your existing position is worth once every study is lined up together.

Why scaling exists

Studies attract different groups of students. Without a correction, a VCE aggregate would partly reflect which studies you chose rather than how you performed in them.

Scaling removes that. It is what allows VTAC to place a Specialist Mathematics student and a broad-entry student on the same footing.

How scaling works

VTAC examines how each study's students performed across all of their studies, not just the one being scaled. A study whose students are consistently strong elsewhere is scaled up; a study with a broader cohort is moved down.

The judgement concerns the group, never the individual. Your rank inside your study, already fixed by your study score, does not move.

How much does scaling move a result?

The size of the shift depends on the study and the year. Victoria publishes this more openly than most states, which is why VCE scaling is so widely discussed — and so widely misread.

A published table shows what happened to last year's cohort. It does not promise what will happen to yours.

Subjects that scale up

In the VCE, Specialist Mathematics, Latin, Chemistry, high-level languages and Mathematical Methods scale strongly. The shared factor is intake, not difficulty: these studies attract students who perform well across their whole VCE.

The study is not being rewarded for being demanding. Its cohort is being recognised for being strong.

Subjects that scale down

Broad-entry studies tend to scale down. Further Mathematics is the best-known Victorian example, precisely because it draws such a wide cohort.

That does not make it a bad choice. A study that scales modestly still yields a strong scaled score if you rank near the top of it — and a high study score in Further Mathematics beats a mediocre one in Specialist.

Why hard does not mean high-scaling

The belief that hard studies scale up is nearly right, which is why it persists. Demanding studies often do scale up — but not for being demanding. They scale up because of who takes them.

Hand a hard study a broad intake and it would scale down, with its content unchanged.

A concrete scaling example

Consider two VCE students, each with the same strong study score. One sat a study with a strong cohort, the other a broad-entry study. After VTAC scales them, the first score rises and the second eases back.

Neither was rewarded or punished. Each was measured against the students they sat with.

Should you choose for scaling or for results?

Choose for results. The Victorian structure makes this concrete: your top four count in full, so a study you struggle in that lands in your Primary Four does real damage — regardless of how well it scales for other people.

Take studies you can rank highly in. A high study score in a modest scaler regularly beats a low one in a strong scaler.

Why scaling feels unfair but is not

Scaling feels unfair because your score moves after the work is done, on a basis outside your control. That reaction is understandable.

What it delivers, though, is fairness. Without it, your aggregate would partly record your study selection. Scaling makes your four counted studies comparable to everyone else's.

Does scaling change each year?

Yes. VTAC recalculates scaling every year against that year's cohorts, so a study's treatment moves between years. The broad pattern is stable; the exact figures shift.

This is why choosing studies from last year's published report is a mistake. That data described a cohort that was not yours.

Scaling in one idea

Scaling asks how strong the group taking a study was. Victoria then counts four scaled scores in full, adds a tenth of two more, and reads the total against a scale of roughly 210.

Scaling and English

English is scaled like any other study, against the strength of its own cohort. It receives no fixed advantage — though an English study must be in your calculation, which makes your rank in it unavoidable rather than optional.

Small-cohort subjects and scaling

Small cohorts do not break scaling and do not open a loophole. VTAC scales a small study the same way as a large one: by looking at how its students performed across everything else.

Some small studies — high-level languages, Latin — scale strongly, and the reason is their intake, not their size.

Scaling and your best results

Victoria's counting rule is where scaling lands hardest. Your best four scaled scores count in full; your fifth and sixth contribute a tenth each.

Because scaling determines which studies are your best four, it can move a study in or out of the group that counts fully — which matters far more than the increments do.

Check how any subject scales

Rather than trusting a rumour, check a study directly. Run your own scores through the VCE ATAR calculator and the real size of the effect becomes visible.

Scaling is not the only factor

Scaling is not the only factor. An English study must be in your calculation, and individual courses set their own prerequisites no matter how anything scales.

A strong aggregate will not admit you to a course whose prerequisite study you never took.

The takeaway on scaling

Scaling records the strength of your study's cohort, and nothing about you personally. You cannot game it. Rank as high as you can in four studies you can genuinely perform in, and the aggregate follows.

Common questions

What is VCE scaling and why does it exist?

VCE scaling adjusts each subject so results can be compared fairly across different subjects. It exists so your ATAR reflects your ability, not just your subject choice, since some subjects attract stronger cohorts than others.

Do harder subjects scale higher?

Not automatically. Scaling reflects how strong a subject’s cohort is across all subjects, not how difficult the content is. A hard subject with a mixed cohort can scale modestly.

How much does scaling move a mark?

It varies by subject. A strong-scaling subject can lift a result, while a broad-entry subject can lower it a little. The effect is larger for the strongest-scaling subjects.

Does scaling change each year?

Yes. Scaling is recalculated each year based on that year’s cohorts, so a subject’s scaling can shift slightly. The broad pattern is stable, but the exact figures change.
