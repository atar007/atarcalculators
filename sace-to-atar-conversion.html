How SACE marks convert to ATAR

Your SACE results and your ATAR are not the same thing. This guide walks through exactly how your SACE scaled scores turn into an ATAR, step by step, in plain English.

Key takeaways

  • Your ATAR is a rank from 0.00 to 99.95, not the average of your SACE results.
  • SATAC scales each subject, based on how strong its cohort is.
  • Your best scaled Stage 2 results form your aggregate.
  • That aggregate is ranked against your age group to give your ATAR.
  • the SACE Board runs the South Australian Certificate of Education; SATAC works out your ATAR.
  • Each subject is around 70% school assessment and 30% external.
  • You cannot average your marks to get your ATAR.

A rank, not an average

The first thing to understand is that your ATAR is a rank. An ATAR of 85 does not mean your marks averaged 85. It means you finished ahead of about 85% of your age group.

Because it is a rank, it lets universities compare students who took completely different subjects. That is the whole point of the ATAR. Your results feed into it, but they are not it.

Keeping this distinction clear makes everything else about the SACE and the ATAR easier to follow. Marks describe subjects; the ATAR ranks students.

What the SACE is

The SACE is your certificate; the ATAR is a rank derived from it. You need 90 credits at Stage 2, and the Research Project is compulsory whether or not it enters your aggregate.

How a subject result is built

Your SACE result is a set of Stage 2 subject grades. Roughly 70% comes from school assessment and about 30% from external assessment — a different split from most states.

How your assessment is handled

Stage 2 subjects are assessed by your school and moderated by the SACE Board, with an external component. Both halves feed the scaled score SATAC works with.

The Research Project and the NT

The SACE includes a compulsory Research Project, which most students complete before their final Stage 2 year.

The same SACE Board and SATAC arrangements also serve the Northern Territory, so NT students follow the same path to an ATAR.

Step 1: Your subjects are scaled

Once you have your scaled scores, SATAC scales them. Scaling adjusts each subject so that a result in one subject is worth the same as the same result in another.

It works by looking at how the students in a subject perform across all their subjects. If the group is strong overall, the subject scales up. If the group is broad, it scales down.

Crucially, your position within the subject never changes. Scaling shifts the whole subject up or down; it only changes how your result counts towards your ATAR.

Why scaling exists

Scaling exists to be fair. Without it, a student who took easier subjects would have an advantage over one who took harder, more competitive subjects.

Scaling removes that edge. It means no student is better or worse off simply because of the subjects they chose. That fairness is the whole reason it exists.

This is why the idea that “hard subjects scale up” is only half true. A subject scales up because its students are strong, not because the content is difficult. See SACE scaling explained.

Step 2: Your aggregate

Your SACE aggregate takes your best three scaled scores in full plus half of a fourth — the 0.5 rule. The maximum is 90, the smallest aggregate scale in the country.

The English requirement

The SACE has literacy requirements you must satisfy to complete the certificate. Check them early: they gate the certificate, which gates the ATAR.

Step 3: Your aggregate becomes a rank

The final step orders every South Australian aggregate out of 90 and converts your position into a rank out of 99.95.

A worked example

Work the example with South Australian numbers: three subjects in full, one at half, aggregate out of 90. With so few results, each one visibly moves the total.

Subject results are not your ATAR

A scaled score is not an ATAR. Your aggregate out of 90 becomes a percentile only after SATAC ranks the whole cohort.

Who does what in South Australia

Two bodies are involved. the SACE Board of South Australia (the SACE Board) runs the SACE: it sets your subjects, runs the assessment, and gives you your results.

the South Australian Tertiary Admissions Centre (SATAC) takes those results, scales them, and works out your ATAR. So the SACE Board gives you your SACE; SATAC gives you your ATAR. See SATAC explained.

How many subjects should you take?

South Australia counts three-and-a-half subjects. That is fewer than anywhere except the ACT, and it is why one weak result is so expensive here.

Turning your estimate into a plan

Plan around your best three. The half-weighted fourth is a genuine cushion, and it is half — not a spare.

What scaling does to a result

The 0.5 rule means your fourth subject counts for half its scaled value. That is far more than Victoria’s 10% increments and far less than a full subject.

Why you cannot game the system

You cannot game SACE scaling, because it follows your rank within a subject. A high-scaling subject you sit mid-pack in is worse than a strong result elsewhere.

Subject results are not band labels

South Australia has no bands. Stage 2 grades and an aggregate out of 90 — if someone mentions a Band 6, they are describing NSW.

Why you cannot calculate it exactly

No published table converts Stage 2 grades into an exact ATAR, because scaling moves with each year’s cohort. Any tool gives an estimate.

Your ATAR in one idea

If you take one idea from this: the SACE gives you Stage 2 results, and SATAC turns three-and-a-half of them into a rank.

The balance of coursework and exams

The two halves are separate: the SACE Board runs your certificate, SATAC calculates the ATAR. SATAC also serves the Northern Territory.

What this means for choosing subjects

The takeaway: three subjects carry your aggregate, the fourth counts half, and the Research Project is compulsory regardless.

Common questions

How are SACE results converted to an ATAR?

SATAC scales your SACE scaled scores to account for each subject’s cohort strength, combines them into an aggregate, and ranks that against your age group to give an ATAR from 0.00 to 99.95.

What is scaling in the SACE?

Scaling adjusts each subject so the same result means the same thing across subjects, based on how strong the group taking it was. It decides how much each of your results contributes to your ATAR.

How many subjects count for the SACE ATAR?

Your ATAR comes from your best scaled Stage 2 results, combined into a university aggregate by SATAC. Most students complete a full Stage 2 load, including the compulsory Research Project.

Why isn't my ATAR the average of my marks?

Because the ATAR is a rank, not an average. Your scaled results are combined and ranked against your age group, so your ATAR reflects your position, not the arithmetic mean of your results.
