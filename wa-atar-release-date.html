WA ATAR release date 2026: when do results come out?

Waiting on your WACE results? This guide covers the timing you can expect for WA ATARs in 2026, how TISC delivers them, and when university offers follow.

By the ATARCalculators Editorial Team ·Reviewed 3 July 2026 ·Updated July 2026 ·6 min read

When are 2026 WA ATAR results released?

WA ATAR results come out each year in late December. Based on recent years, expect them in the second half of December 2026.

SCSA and TISC set and announce the exact date closer to the time. So treat late December as a guide, not a fixed promise. A day or two of movement year to year is normal.

The safest move is to check the official TISC and SCSA pages in November or December 2026 for the confirmed date and time.

What time of day are WA results out?

Results are usually available from early in the morning on release day. TISC publishes the exact time alongside the date, so check its page for the confirmed release time.

It is normal for the portal to be busy right when results go live. If the site is slow, wait a short while and try again, rather than refreshing over and over.

How will I receive my WA ATAR?

Your ATAR is released by TISC through its secure online portal. You log in with the account you used to apply.

Your WACE subject results come from SCSA around the same time, through its student portal. Keep both logins handy before results day, so you are not hunting for them on the morning.

WACE results and ATAR are separate

It helps to know these are two different things. Your WACE subject results show how you did in each course. Your ATAR is the rank TISC works out from your best four scaled scores.

You get your WACE results from SCSA and your ATAR from TISC. They arrive around the same time, but from different places. See our WA ATAR guide for how the two connect.

When do university offers follow?

After ATARs are released, universities make offers in rounds. First-round offers usually arrive in January, with later rounds for courses that still have places.

If you do not get your first choice in the first round, keep watching. Later rounds and pathway options are still worth considering. Use the time to review your preferences.

How to prepare for results day

A little preparation makes results day calmer. Find your TISC and SCSA logins a few days early, and check they work.

Decide where you want to be when results arrive, and who you want around you. Some students like company, others prefer to check alone first.

Have your course preferences and a backup plan ready. That way, whatever your ATAR, you know your next step straight away.

What if there is a mistake in your results?

If a result looks wrong, you have options. SCSA runs processes to check and, in some cases, review marks.

There are deadlines for asking for a review, and sometimes a fee, so act quickly if something seems off. Start on the SCSA website, which explains the steps.

Most results are correct, so try not to worry in advance. Just know the option exists if you need it.

What to do while you wait

The wait is stressful, so plan for it. Have a backup course in mind, in case your first choice needs a higher ATAR than you get.

It also helps to know your options early. Read our WA cutoffs guide so you know which courses suit a range of results. That way, whatever your ATAR, you have a plan ready.

What happens after your ATAR

Your ATAR is not the end of the process. After results, you finalise or adjust your course preferences through TISC, and first-round university offers follow in January. So there is a gap between getting your ATAR and receiving an offer.

Use that gap wisely. Once you know your ATAR, review your preference list and make sure your genuine first choice sits at the top, with realistic options below it. You can usually adjust preferences before the offer round, which is worth doing if your ATAR differs from what you expected.

If your results are not what you hoped

If your ATAR is lower than you hoped, remember it is one pathway among many. WA universities offer alternative entry options, bridging and enabling courses, and pathways through TAFE that can lead to a degree. An unexpected result is a setback, not a dead end.

Take a little time, then look at your options calmly. Many students reach the course they want through a second pathway, and universities have staff who can talk you through the alternatives. There is almost always a route forward.
