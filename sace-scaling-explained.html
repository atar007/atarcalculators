SACE scaling explained

Scaling is the most misunderstood part of the SACE. This guide explains what it is, why it exists, and how it decides which subjects help your ATAR most.

SACE scaling is the step where SATAC puts every Stage 2 subject on a common footing before your aggregate is built. Because South Australia counts only your best three subjects in full plus half of a fourth, each scaled result carries more weight here than in any other state. Scaling responds to how strong a subject's cohort was, not to how demanding the content felt, and it never moves you past anyone you finished behind.

Key takeaways

  • SATAC scales every Stage 2 subject before your aggregate is assembled.
  • South Australia counts three subjects in full plus half of a fourth, so each scaled result carries unusual weight.
  • A subject scales up when its cohort is academically strong, not when its content is hard.
  • Scaling adjusts what your result is worth, never your rank inside the subject.
  • The aggregate tops out at 90, and scaling decides how close you get.
  • There is no combination of subjects that beats simply ranking well in what you take.

What is SACE scaling?

Scaling is the conversion SATAC applies to each Stage 2 subject so that a result in one subject means the same thing as the same result in another. It happens after your results are finalised, on the way to your aggregate.

In South Australia the stakes are unusually concentrated. Your aggregate is built from just three subjects counted in full plus half of a fourth, out of a maximum of 90. With so few results carrying the load, the scaling applied to each one has more room to move your final rank than it would in a system counting five or ten.

Why scaling exists

Students do not all take the same subjects, and subjects do not all attract the same students. Without a correction, your ATAR would partly record which subjects you happened to choose rather than how well you did.

Scaling removes that distortion. It is the mechanism that lets SATAC compare a Stage 2 student who took Specialist Mathematics against one who took a broad-entry subject, and treat both fairly.

How scaling works

SATAC examines how the students in each subject performed across all of their subjects, not just the one being scaled. If a subject's students are consistently strong elsewhere, that subject's results are lifted. If the cohort is broad, results are moved down.

The judgement is about the group, never about you. Your standing within your subject is fixed by your own result and stays exactly where it was.

How much does scaling move a result?

The shift varies by subject and by year, and in South Australia it is amplified by the structure. When only three and a half results build a total out of 90, a few points of scaling movement on a single subject translates into a visible change in your aggregate.

That is worth knowing, and it is not worth panicking about. The movement follows the cohort, and the cohort follows the subject, so the pattern is broadly predictable from year to year.

Subjects that scale up

In the SACE, Mathematics Specialist, Chemistry, Physics and high-level languages scale strongly. What these subjects share is not difficulty but intake: the students who choose them tend to perform well across their whole SACE.

That is the entire mechanism. The subject is not being rewarded for being hard. Its cohort is being recognised for being strong.

Subjects that scale down

Broad-entry subjects tend to scale down, along with some practical subjects. That is a statement about who enrols, not about what the subject is worth.

And it does not make them poor choices. Because South Australia counts so few results, a subject you rank near the top of can out-earn a high-scaling subject you sit mid-pack in. A modest scaler at the top of the range is a genuinely good outcome.

Why hard does not mean high-scaling

The belief that hard subjects scale up survives because it is nearly right. Demanding subjects often do scale up — but not because they are demanding. They scale up because they attract students who do well everywhere.

If a hard subject were taken tomorrow by a broad cohort, it would scale down. The content would not have changed at all.

A concrete scaling example

Take two SACE students with the same strong Stage 2 result. One sat a subject with a strong cohort, the other a broad-entry subject. After SATAC scales them, the first result rises and the second eases back.

Neither student was rewarded or punished. Each was measured against the group they sat with — and in a system where three and a half results build a total out of 90, that difference lands with real force.

Should you choose for scaling or for results?

Choose for results. In South Australia this advice is stronger than elsewhere, because the aggregate is so concentrated. Three subjects carry your total, so one subject you struggle in does real damage — and a high-scaling subject you struggle in is still a subject you struggle in.

Take the subjects you can rank highly in. Scaling will do its work on a strong result far more usefully than on a weak one.

Why scaling feels unfair but is not

Scaling feels unfair when your result moves after you have finished, on a basis you had no control over. That reaction makes sense.

What it protects, though, is you. Without it, a SACE student's rank would partly reflect their subject selection. Scaling is the step that makes your three and a half counted results comparable to everyone else's, whatever they chose.

Does scaling change each year?

Yes. SATAC recalculates scaling every year against that year's cohorts, so a subject's treatment can drift from one year to the next. The broad pattern is stable; the exact figures are not.

This is precisely why chasing last year's numbers is a poor strategy. You would be selecting subjects using data that will not apply to your own cohort.

Scaling in one idea

Scaling asks one question: how strong was the group that took this subject? Everything else follows from the answer. In South Australia, the answer matters more than in most states, because so few results build your aggregate.

Scaling and English

English follows the same rule as everything else: it is scaled against the strength of its own cohort. There is no special treatment, and no English subject is scaled to be worth more than another by decree.

What matters is your rank within whichever English subject you take.

Small-cohort subjects and scaling

Small cohorts do not break scaling, and they do not create a loophole. SATAC scales a small subject the same way as a large one: by looking at how its students performed across their other subjects.

A small subject with a very strong intake will scale up. A small subject with a broad intake will not. Size on its own decides nothing.

Scaling and your best results

South Australia's counting rule is where scaling really bites. Your best three scaled results count in full and half of a fourth is added, giving an aggregate out of 90.

Because scaling decides which of your results are the best three, it does not only change your total — it can change which subjects make the cut at all.

Scaling is not the only factor

Scaling is not the whole picture. You need 90 credits of study at Stage 2, and individual courses carry their own prerequisites regardless of how anything scales.

A perfectly scaled aggregate will not admit you to a course whose prerequisite you never sat.

The takeaway on scaling

Scaling recognises the strength of your subject's cohort, and nothing about you personally. You cannot outmanoeuvre it, and in South Australia — where three and a half results carry everything — you do not need to. Rank as high as you can in subjects you can actually perform in, and the scaling looks after itself.

Common questions

What is SACE scaling and why does it exist?

SACE scaling adjusts each subject so results can be compared fairly across different subjects. It exists so your ATAR reflects your ability, not just your subject choice, since some subjects attract stronger cohorts than others.

Do harder subjects scale higher?

Not automatically. Scaling reflects how strong a subject’s cohort is across all subjects, not how difficult the content is. A hard subject with a mixed cohort can scale modestly.

How much does scaling move a mark?

It varies by subject. A strong-scaling subject can lift a result, while a broad-entry subject can lower it a little. The effect is larger for the strongest-scaling subjects.

Does scaling change each year?

Yes. Scaling is recalculated each year based on that year’s cohorts, so a subject’s scaling can shift slightly. The broad pattern is stable, but the exact figures change.
