WACE scaling explained

Scaling is the most misunderstood part of the WACE. This guide explains what it is, why it exists, and how it decides which subjects help your ATAR most.

WACE scaling is the step where TISC adjusts each ATAR course mark so courses can be compared. Western Australia then adds your best four scaled marks and applies a further 10% bonus for Mathematics Methods, Mathematics Specialist and languages, building a TEA out of 430. Scaling responds to the strength of a course's cohort rather than the difficulty of its content, and never changes your rank inside your own course.

Key takeaways

  • TISC scales every ATAR course mark before your TEA is built.
  • Only ATAR courses count — General courses do not feed the TEA.
  • WA adds your best four scaled marks, then a 10% bonus for Methods, Specialist and LOTE.
  • A course scales up when its cohort is academically strong, not when its content is hard.
  • The TEA tops out at 430 — four scaled marks plus the bonuses.
  • No course combination beats simply ranking well in what you take.

What is WACE scaling?

Scaling is the conversion TISC applies to each ATAR course so that a mark in one course means the same as the same mark in another. It runs after your results are settled, on the way to your TEA.

Western Australia adds a distinctive second step. Once scaling is done, an explicit bonus is applied to certain courses — which makes WA the state where what happens after scaling matters most.

Why scaling exists

Students take different courses, and courses attract different cohorts. Without a correction, your TEA would partly record your course selection rather than your performance.

Scaling removes that distortion, letting TISC compare a Physics student and a broad-entry student on equal terms.

How scaling works

TISC examines how each course's students performed across all of their courses, not only the one being scaled. Students who are consistently strong elsewhere lift their course's scaling; a broader cohort moves it down.

The judgement is about the group. Your standing inside your own course is fixed by your own mark and does not move.

How much does scaling move a result?

How far a mark moves depends on the course and the year. In WA there is a second layer to keep in view: after scaling, Mathematics Methods, Mathematics Specialist and languages attract an additional 10% of their scaled score.

So two effects stack — the scaling itself, and then the bonus. Reading only the first understates what those courses actually contribute.

Subjects that scale up

In the WACE, Mathematics Specialist, Chemistry, Physics, Mathematics Methods and high-level languages scale strongly. The common thread is intake: their students tend to do well across their whole WACE.

The course is not being credited for being hard. Its cohort is being recognised for being strong.

Subjects that scale down

Broad-entry courses tend to scale down, along with some practical and vocational courses. That is a fact about enrolment, not about worth.

It does not make them poor choices either. A course that scales modestly still delivers a strong scaled mark if you rank near the top of it.

Why hard does not mean high-scaling

The claim that hard courses scale up is close enough to true to survive. Demanding courses often do scale up — but the difficulty is not the reason. The cohort is.

Give a hard course a broad intake and it would scale down, with its content untouched.

A concrete scaling example

Take two WA students with the same strong mark. One sat a course with a strong cohort, the other a broad-entry course. After TISC scales them, the first mark rises and the second eases back.

Neither was rewarded or punished. Each was measured against the students they sat with — and if the first course happened to be Methods or Specialist, a further 10% follows on top.

Should you choose for scaling or for results?

Choose for results. WA's bonus system tempts students into Methods or Specialist for the extra 10%, but 10% of a weak scaled mark is a weak number. The bonus multiplies your result; it does not replace it.

Take courses you can rank highly in. If those happen to include Methods, Specialist or a language, the bonus is a genuine reward rather than a rescue.

Why scaling feels unfair but is not

Scaling feels unfair because your mark shifts after the work is finished, on a basis you did not control. That is a fair reaction.

What it protects is comparability. Without it, a WA TEA would reward convenient course selection over actual achievement.

Does scaling change each year?

Yes. TISC recalculates scaling every year against that year's cohorts, so a course's treatment drifts between years. The broad pattern is stable; the exact figures are not.

Choosing courses from last year's numbers means planning against a cohort that was not yours.

Scaling in one idea

Scaling asks how strong the group taking a course was. WA then adds four scaled marks, applies the Mathematics and LOTE bonuses, and reads the total against a scale that reaches 430.

Scaling and English

English is scaled against the strength of its own cohort, like any other course. It attracts no fixed advantage and no bonus.

What carries through is your rank within the English course you take.

Small-cohort subjects and scaling

Small cohorts neither break scaling nor create a loophole. TISC scales a small course exactly as it scales a large one, by looking at how its students performed elsewhere.

Some small courses — high-level languages in particular — scale strongly and also attract the 10% bonus. The reason is their intake, not their size.

Scaling and your best results

Your best four scaled marks form the base of the TEA, and the bonuses are added on top. Anything past the fourth drops out of the base entirely.

Because scaling decides which marks are your best four, it can move a course in or out of the group that counts at all.

Scaling is not the only factor

Scaling is not the only thing that matters. ATAR-course status decides whether a course counts at all, and individual university courses set their own prerequisites regardless of scaling or bonuses.

A strong TEA will not admit you to a course whose prerequisite you never sat.

The takeaway on scaling

Scaling reflects the strength of your course's cohort, and nothing about you personally. You cannot outmanoeuvre it, and the bonus will not save a weak mark. Rank as high as you can in four ATAR courses you can genuinely perform in, and the TEA follows.

Common questions

What is WACE scaling and why does it exist?

WACE scaling adjusts each subject so results can be compared fairly across different subjects. It exists so your ATAR reflects your ability, not just your subject choice, since some subjects attract stronger cohorts than others.

Do harder subjects scale higher?

Not automatically. Scaling reflects how strong a subject’s cohort is across all subjects, not how difficult the content is. A hard subject with a mixed cohort can scale modestly.

How much does scaling move a mark?

It varies by subject. A strong-scaling subject can lift a result, while a broad-entry subject can lower it a little. The effect is larger for the strongest-scaling subjects.

Does scaling change each year?

Yes. Scaling is recalculated each year based on that year’s cohorts, so a subject’s scaling can shift slightly. The broad pattern is stable, but the exact figures change.
